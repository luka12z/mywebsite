The Picture of Dorian Gray

Oscar Wilde

Rating: ★★★★★

"The Picture of Dorian Gray" is a classic novel written by Oscar Wilde and first published in 1890. The story revolves around Dorian Gray, a young and attractive man living in London. Dorian's portrait is painted by an artist named Basil Hallward, and during the painting process, Dorian makes a wish that he would remain young and beautiful while the portrait ages instead. As time passes, Dorian becomes influenced by Lord Henry Wotton, a hedonistic and cynical character. Dorian's pursuit of pleasure and beauty leads him down a morally destructive path, while his portrait bears the consequences of his immoral actions, aging and reflecting the corruption of his soul. The novel explores themes of morality, the consequences of hedonism, and the corrupting influence of aestheticism. It's a cautionary tale about the consequences of living a life solely focused on superficial beauty and pleasure.

Crime and Punishment

Fyodor Dostoevsky

Crime and Punishment" is a novel written by Fyodor Dostoevsky, published in 1866. The book is a psychological exploration of the consequences of crime and the internal struggles faced by its protagonist, Rodion Raskolnikov. Here's a rough description of the novel: Set in St. Petersburg, the story follows Rodion Raskolnikov, a former student who is impoverished and intellectually ambitious. Convinced of his own superiority and influenced by nihilistic ideas, Raskolnikov hatches a plan to commit a murder to prove his theory that certain extraordinary individuals have the right to commit crimes for the greater good. However, the aftermath of the murder sets off a chain of events that profoundly affects Raskolnikov's conscience and mental state. The novel delves into the psychological torment experienced by Raskolnikov as he grapples with guilt, paranoia, and the moral implications of his actions. The narrative is interwoven with the lives of other characters, including Sonia, a devout and compassionate woman, and Porfiry, a shrewd detective who suspects Raskolnikov's involvement in the crime. As the plot unfolds, "Crime and Punishment" explores themes of morality, redemption, and the consequences of moral transgressions. Dostoevsky masterfully portrays the human psyche, addressing the complexities of human nature and the potential for spiritual and moral renewal. The novel is a profound exploration of the human condition, making it one of the most significant works in world literature.
